What is Green Infrastructure?

Nature-based solutions for a resilient built environment

Green Infrastructure (GI) refers to the strategic integration of natural systems and engineered solutions designed to address environmental challenges in urban and rural landscapes. It incorporates interconnected networks of natural and constructed features — bioretention areas, green roofs, permeable pavements, constructed wetlands, urban forests, rain gardens, and more — that replicate ecological processes to deliver critical services including stormwater management, water quality improvement, climate adaptation, flood mitigation, urban heat reduction, and enhanced public health.

Unlike traditional "gray infrastructure" such as concrete storm drains, Green Infrastructure works with natural processes rather than against them — managing rainfall at its source, reducing runoff, improving water quality, sequestering carbon, and creating more livable, resilient communities. GI is applied at every scale: from a single rain garden on a residential lot to watershed-wide planning frameworks guiding entire cities and regions.

GI intersects with stormwater engineering, landscape architecture, urban planning, environmental compliance, land development, construction, ecology, public policy, and community development — making it one of the most cross-disciplinary fields in the built environment today.
